# Executive Function — Reference

## Scientific foundation

### Executive function deficit model (Barkley)

Russell Barkley's model posits ADHD as fundamentally a disorder of *behavioral inhibition* — the ability to stop a prepotent response. When inhibition fails, four downstream executive functions are impaired:

1. **Working memory** — holding information in mind and using it to guide behavior
2. **Internalized speech** — self-directed talk that regulates behavior ("Okay, first I need to open the file, then...")
3. **Emotional self-regulation** — managing emotional reactions to maintain goal-directed behavior
4. **Reconstitution** — analysis and synthesis of behavior (planning, problem-solving, generating novel responses)

**Implication for this skill**: Externalize what the brain can't hold internally. Provide structure for self-talk. Help regulate emotional responses to tasks. Break problems down into concrete steps.

Key reference: Barkley, R. A. (1997). *ADHD and the Nature of Self-Control*. Guilford Press.

### Interest-based nervous system (Dodson)

William Dodson's framework identifies five motivational levers for the ADHD brain:
- **Interest** (I want to)
- **Novelty** (this is new)
- **Challenge** (this is hard but winnable)
- **Urgency** (this has a deadline)
- **Passion** (this matters to me)

Conspicuously absent: **Importance** and **Consequences**. Telling an ADHD brain "this is important" or "you'll fail if you don't" does not motivate.

**Implication for this skill**: Reframe tasks around the available levers. A boring code review can become a challenge ("find the three riskiest lines") or a novelty ("use a tool you haven't used before").

Key reference: Dodson, W. (2021). "The ADHD Nervous System." *ADDitude Magazine*.

### Time blindness

ADHD involves impaired time perception across multiple dimensions:
- **Duration estimation** — how long things will take
- **Temporal foresight** — how close a deadline actually feels
- **Time reproduction** — accurately replicating a time interval

Glicksohn & Hadad (2012) showed that individuals with ADHD have difficulty estimating and reproducing time intervals, especially longer ones (>10 seconds). Barkley describes "time blindness" as a failure of temporal foresight — the ability to hold the future in mind.

**Implication for this skill**: Provide time anchoring. Estimate durations explicitly. Check in at intervals. Make abstract timelines concrete ("that's two pomodoros").

### Working memory as prosthetic

Kofler et al. (2018) demonstrated that working memory deficits are a core feature of ADHD, persisting even when hyperactivity/impulsivity remits. External tools (lists, notes, reminders) serve as cognitive prosthetics.

**Implication for this skill**: Never expect the user to hold a plan or state in their head. Write it down. Capture it in Sunsama. Externalize aggressively.

### Default mode network (DMN) intrusion

The DMN is the brain's "resting" network — active during mind-wandering, self-referential thought, and rumination. The task-positive network (TPN) is active during goal-directed work. In neurotypical brains, these networks toggle. In ADHD brains, the DMN intrudes into the TPN, causing lapses in attention.

Sonuga-Barke & Castellanos (2007) proposed the "default-mode interference hypothesis" — ADHD involves difficulty suppressing the DMN when the TPN should be active.

**Implication for this skill**: Gentle external cues can help the network toggle. But shame/stress activates the DMN further (self-referential rumination), so correction must be light and non-judgmental.

Key reference: Sonuga-Barke, E. J. S., & Castellanos, F. X. (2007). "Spontaneous attentional fluctuations in impaired states and pathological conditions: A neurobiological hypothesis." *Neuroscience & Biobehavioral Reviews*, 31(7), 977-986.

### Rejection sensitive dysphoria (RSD)

Dodson describes RSD as an extreme emotional sensitivity to perceived rejection, criticism, or failure — common in ADHD. This means task avoidance can be driven by fear of doing it wrong, not laziness.

**Implication for this skill**: Normalize difficulty. Frame tasks as experiments, not tests. Never evaluate the person — evaluate the approach.
